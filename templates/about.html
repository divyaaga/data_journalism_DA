Car Crashes in New York City

Hi! My project explores the number of car crashes in the different boroughs of NYC over the last ten years. I wanted to see if various external events, like COVID, had significant impacts on the number of car crashes. I also wanted to see if one borough consistently had more car crashes than the others.

How I Got My Data

I first found my dataset from data.gov. Then, I removed all of the car crashes that did not have a borough attached. This means that the data that I haven't presented does not tell the complete story; rather, it is a reflection of all the car crashes that were recorded appropriately for my project. Then I sorted all the car crashes by borough and year and summed the data.

Vocab

Data.gov: An open access US government website containing over 300,000 data sets.

Car Crashes: Any incident involving a collision between at least two vehicles—this collision does not need to be fatal.

Boroughs: NYC is broken up into 5 boroughs: The Bronx, Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens, and Staten Island.

Prototypes

Graph

I chose to make a line graph to show all of the data over the years—I thought this was a good way to display all the data in a straightforward way. I also wanted to make it easy for users to pinpoint a particular year while still being able to understand larger trends.

Some of the feedback I recieved about this design was:

  • Add a button at the bottom allowing users to naviagte to a particular year (Like Life Expectancy)
  • Switch the order of interpretations and takeaways
  • Move both of those sections to below the graph

    • For my final design, I chose to implement all of these changes. I especially thought the suggestion to switch the order of the two sections was helpful, as I think my project now makes way more logical sense.

    Maps

    I chose to make individual maps for each year of data. I liked that users would be able to compare individual boroughs. I had originally chosen to make the colors a greyscale because I thought that that would be the easiest way to tell the shades apart, but I wanted to include colors on the outline of the boroughs to match the line graph colors.

    Some of the feedback I recieved about this design was:

  • Don't use greyscale—you can still use a more neutral color, but choose another option because grey is not engaging
  • Instead of leaving Long Island/Upstate New York uncolored, either make it grey or cut it completely. Also, if you have those regions, include New Jersey as well.
  • Get rid of the airports—they are distracting and add nothing.

    • For my final design, I chose to implement most of these changes. I decided to include the surrounding regions, but keep them grey. I also chose to make the color scale more brownish instead of grey.
